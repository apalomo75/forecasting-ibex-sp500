06 Directional Risk Forecasting (Random Forest)

This notebook transitions from volatility and tail-risk modelling to predictive risk classification, applying machine learning to forecast the direction of market movements for the IBEX 35 and S&P 500 indices.

Building upon the econometric foundation laid in 05 VaR & Expected Shortfall, this module integrates both technical indicators and **volatility-derived features (from EGARCH models) to train a Random Forest classifier that predicts whether the next daily return will be positive or negative, translating continuous risk measures into directional trading signals.

Machine learning leverages nonlinear interactions among predictors such as RSI, MACD, momentum, lagged returns, and a VIX proxy to enhance short-term predictive power.

Performance is evaluated through confusion matrices, Binomial directionality tests, and Sharpe Ratios derived from simulated long/short strategies. Together, these analyses assess whether a hybrid econometricML framework can anticipate directional risk more effectively than purely statistical forecasting.

Objectives

  • Predict directional returns (up/down) using Random Forest and XGBoost models.
  • Integrate EGARCH-derived volatility and technical indicators into the feature space.
  • Evaluate model accuracy via confusion matrices, Binomial Test, and Sharpe Ratio.
  • Compare predictive performance across the IBEX 35 and S&P 500.
  • Visualize model-based equity curves and time-varying classification performance.

STEP 1 Data Integration & Feature Assembly

This step builds the clean, unified dataset that will feed the directional forecasting models (Random Forest / XGBoost). It integrates three independent information sources:

  1. Daily returns for IBEX 35 and S&P 500
  2. Econometric volatility from EGARCH(1,1)-t
  3. Technical indicators (RSI, MACD, Momentum, Rolling Volatility)

The goal is to produce a stable, fully-aligned, NaN-controlled dataset that captures both price dynamics and risk regimes, before lag features and targets are created in Step 2.

1. Daily Returns

Daily log-returns for IBEX and SPX form the backbone of the feature matrix and will later generate lagged features to model short-term autocorrelation and momentum.

2. EGARCH Conditional Volatility (Vol_IBEX, Vol_SPX)

These variables represent the time-varying risk estimated from an EGARCH(1,1)-t model, able to capture:

  • Volatility clustering
  • Asymmetric reaction to negative shocks
  • Long memory in variance
  • Heavy-tailed return behaviour

Interpretation:

  • Low volatility ( 0.60.8) stable regime, smoother and more predictable returns
  • High volatility ( 1.01.5) turbulent regime, higher uncertainty, weaker predictability
  • Volatility spikes often precede regime shifts (trend->chaos, or chaos->normalization)

EGARCH volatility enriches the dataset with risk-state information that traditional technical indicators do not capture.

3. Technical Indicators

RSI (Relative Strength Index)

A bounded 0100 measure of recent price momentum based on the ratio of gains vs. losses.

Interpretation thresholds:

  • RSI < 30 oversold conditions, potential rebound
  • RSI 50 neutral regime, market directionless
  • RSI > 70 overbought conditions, potential correction

It captures short-term reversal pressure and market stretching.

MACD (Moving Average Convergence Divergence)

Difference between short-term (12-day) and long-term (26-day) EMAs, smoothed by a 9-day signal line.

Interpretation:

  • MACD > 0 bullish momentum
  • MACD < 0 bearish momentum
  • Increasing MACD trend acceleration
  • Decreasing MACD trend weakening

It captures trend direction and speed of change.

Momentum (5-day difference)

Simple comparison between todays price and the price 5 days ago.

  • Positive momentum short-term upward pressure
  • Negative momentum short-term downward pressure

Very effective for detecting local continuation patterns.

Rolling Volatility (20-day realised volatility)

The 20-day standard deviation of returns, annualised.

  • Low volatility (<10%) calm market, cleaner directional signals
  • High volatility (>2025%) noisy market, higher probability of whipsaws

Provides a second volatility dimension complementary to EGARCH: one is econometric (model-based), the other empirical (realised).

How the model learn to read the direction

The model will learn directional signals from a combination of:

1. Price behaviour (RSI, MACD, Momentum) Captures trend, acceleration, and short-term reversals.

2. Risk regime (EGARCH + rolling volatility) Determines whether the market is predictable (low vol) or chaotic (high vol).

3. Regime interactions For example:

  • RSI < 30 works well in low-vol regimes, fails in high vol
  • MACD crossovers are more reliable when EGARCH volatility is stable
  • Momentum signals behave differently depending on volatility spikes

By assembling both momentum and volatility information, Step 1 creates the complete economic context in which the model will make directional predictions.

STEP 2 Building the Predictive Framework: Lagged Feature Engineering + Direction Label Construction

Here, it transforms a single daily snapshot of market conditions into a multi-day temporal window, adding structure, memory, and context that the model needs in order to forecast next-day direction. This step explains how the market has been moving in the last few days.

This step is essential because financial markets exhibit momentum, reversal, persistence, and regime transitions, all of which depend on short-term historical patterns.

Main Goal

The idea is to construct a dataset that captures:

  • recent trends
  • momentum shifts
  • acceleration and deceleration of signals
  • how technical indicators evolve over multiple days
  • how short-term volatility patterns unfold

To achieve this, Step 2 performs two major operations:

Generate 1-day, 2-day, and 3-day Lagged Features

Each row in the dataset is expanded so it no longer contains information only for that day, but also information from the previous three trading days for every indicator:

  • Returns
  • EGARCH volatility
  • RSI
  • MACD
  • Momentum

Example (IBEX):

Feature Meaning
IBEX todays return
IBEX_lag1 yesterdays return
IBEX_lag2 return 2 days ago
IBEX_lag3 return 3 days ago
RSI_IBEX_lag1 RSI as of yesterday
Vol_IBEX_lag2 EGARCH volatility from 2 days ago
etc.

Lags matter

Because the market rarely reacts to a single day in isolation.

With lagged features, the model can detect:

  • reversals
    RSI sinking for several days potential turning point
  • trend continuation
    momentum positive for 23 days sustained upward pressure
  • volatility regime changes
    EGARCH rising multiple days risk of instability and whipsaws
  • trend acceleration
    MACD rising over multiple lags growing trend strength

Without lagged features, the model cannot understand the trajectory of indicators only their value today.

Create the Binary Target Variable: direction

The notebook defines the label the model will predict: direction = 1 if tomorrows return > 0 direction = 0 if tomorrows return < 0

Why shift returns one day into the future to ensure the model always predicts forward, avoiding any information leakage from the present.

This label allows the model to learn patterns like:

  • when recent conditions tend to produce an upward move
  • when the market is more likely to fall
  • when volatility disrupts the reliability of signals
  • how multi-day sequences of indicators behave before a directional change

Directional Prediction

The predictive information lies in **how RSI, MACD, Momentum, and Volatility values have been evolving, and Step 2 enables the model to learn short-term sequences such as:

  • Reversion patterns
    RSI < 30 today and yesterday + falling momentum rebound likely
  • Continuation patterns
    momentum positive for 3 days trend continuation
  • Risk regime transitions
    EGARCH increasing for 23 days unstable environment
  • Trend strength or exhaustion
    MACD rising for several days accelerating trend

With lags, the model sees movement instead of isolated values, and the second step converts the dataset from a static daily snapshot into a dynamic temporal model of market behaviour. By embedding memory, short-term evolution, and directional labels, it provides the structural foundation that enables the machine learning model to forecast tomorrows direction.

STEP 3 Training and Evaluating the Directional Forecasting Model (Random Forest & XGBoost)

Here, machine learning models (Random Forest and XGBoost) are trained to anticipate whether the next days return will be Up (1) or Down (0) for both the IBEX 35 and the S&P 500.

Using the lagged features from Step 2 and the technical/econometric indicators from Step 1, this step tests whether daily market direction contains exploitable predictive patterns.

Target Definition (Next-Day Direction)

The objective is to predict the sign of tomorrows return, converting the continuous log-return series into a binary classification problem.

The target variable is defined as:

$$ y_{t+1} = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if } r_{t+1} > 0, \\ 0 & \text{if } r_{t+1} \le 0. \end{cases} $$

This transforms daily market behavior into an up/down decision, allowing Random Forest and XGBoost to model directional patterns.

1. Train/Test Dataset Preparation

The dataset is split chronologically, preserving temporal order:

  • Train set: early portion of the series
  • Test set: most recent observations

This prevents information leakage and ensures that predictions are evaluated strictly out-of-sample.

IBEX Train: 5364 rows, Test: 1341 rows SPX Train: 5364 rows, Test: 1341 rows

2. Models Training

I have decided to use two models because of the benefits that each one present:

Random Forest

  • robust to noise
  • capable of capturing non-linear interactions
  • handles correlated indicators well

XGBoost

  • state-of-the-art gradient boosting algorithm
  • strong performance in complex, noisy data
  • excellent at modelling subtle interactions

Both models use the full set of 17 lagged and technical features.

3. Nature of the Evaluation Tests

1) Accuracy

The share of days correctly classified as UP or DOWN. A random guess yields 50%, which serves as the benchmark.

2) Confusion Matrix

Reveals:

  • whether the model predicts UP vs DOWN asymmetrically
  • biases toward a particular class
  • whether the model collapses into predicting only one direction

3) Cross-Model Consistency

If both RF and XGB perform similarly, it indicates that the underlying data does not contain learnable directional structure.

4. Results

# STEP 4  EVALUATION & BACKTESTING

import numpy as np
import pandas as pd
from scipy.stats import binomtest
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# 4.1 Binomial Directionality Test
print(" Running Binomial Directionality Test...")

def binomial_direction_test(y_true, y_pred):
    hits = np.sum(y_true == y_pred)
    n = len(y_true)
    p_value = binomtest(hits, n, p=0.5, alternative="greater").pvalue
    hit_ratio = hits / n
    return hit_ratio, p_value

tests = []
for label, y_true, y_pred in [
    ("RF_IBEX", y_test_ibex, rf_ibex_preds),
    ("RF_SPX", y_test_spx, rf_spx_preds),
    ("XGB_IBEX", y_test_ibex, xgb_ibex_preds),
    ("XGB_SPX", y_test_spx, xgb_spx_preds)
]:
    hit_ratio, p_value = binomial_direction_test(y_true, y_pred)
    tests.append({"Model": label, "Hit_Ratio": hit_ratio, "p_value": p_value})
    print(f"{label}: Hit Ratio = {hit_ratio:.3f} | p = {p_value:.4f}")

binom_df = pd.DataFrame(tests)
display(binom_df)

# 4.2 Strategy Simulation (Long/Short)
print("\n Simulating long/short strategies...")

def backtest_strategy(returns, preds):
    """
    Simulate a simple long/short strategy:
    - Go long (1) if predicted direction = 1
    - Go short (-1) if predicted direction = 0
    """
    positions = np.where(preds == 1, 1, -1)
    strat_returns = positions * returns.values
    equity_curve = (1 + strat_returns).cumprod()
    return strat_returns, equity_curve

# Compute strategy returns for each model
ibex_rf_ret, ibex_rf_eq = backtest_strategy(ibex_test["IBEX"], rf_ibex_preds)
spx_rf_ret,  spx_rf_eq  = backtest_strategy(spx_test["SPX"],  rf_spx_preds)
ibex_xgb_ret, ibex_xgb_eq = backtest_strategy(ibex_test["IBEX"], xgb_ibex_preds)
spx_xgb_ret,  spx_xgb_eq  = backtest_strategy(spx_test["SPX"],  xgb_spx_preds)

# 4.3 Compute Sharpe Ratios
def sharpe_ratio(strategy_returns, risk_free=0.0):
    mean_ret = np.mean(strategy_returns - risk_free)
    std_ret = np.std(strategy_returns)
    if std_ret == 0:
        return np.nan
    return (mean_ret / std_ret) * np.sqrt(252)  # annualized (daily freq)

        sharpe_ratio(spx_rf_ret),
        sharpe_ratio(ibex_xgb_ret),
        sharpe_ratio(spx_xgb_ret)

})

STEP 5 Interpretation of Visualisations (Confusion Matrices, Equity Curves, Sharpe Ratios)

5.1 Confusion Matrices

Confusion matrices display how often the model correctly or incorrectly predicts up or down days.

Each matrix shows:

  • True Positive (TP): predicted UP & actual UP
  • True Negative (TN): predicted DOWN & actual DOWN
  • False Positive (FP): predicted UP but actual DOWN
  • False Negative (FN): predicted DOWN but actual UP

A model with real predictive power should show:

  • Strong diagonal dominance (TP & TN much higher than FP & FN)
  • A consistent pattern: e.g., detects upward trends or downward regimes

What we observe for both IBEX and S&P 500:

  • The four blocks are balanced, not diagonal-dominant
  • TP and TN are roughly equal to FP and FN
  • No visible asymmetry or regime sensitivity

This visually confirms what the earlier statistical tests already showed: there is no significant directional predictive ability at the daily horizon.

5.2 Simulated Equity Curve.

Equity curves show the cumulative return over time if we traded using the models directional signals (long for +1, short for 1).

A good directional strategy should show:

  • A smooth upward trajectory
  • Limited drawdowns
  • Stability across different market regimes

What we see for both indices:

  • The curves fluctuate heavily around zero.
  • Subsequent streaks of incorrect signals frequently erase small gains.
  • Performance is unstable, volatile, and highly sensitive to noise.
  • No consistent uptrend emerges for either RF or XGB

Even if hit ratios were slightly >50 %, instability would destroy any usable performance.

5.3 Sharpe Ratio Comparison

The Sharpe ratio evaluates risk-adjusted performance, measuring how much excess return a strategy generates per unit of volatility.
It answers the question: Does this strategy compensate for the risk it takes?

$$ \text{Sharpe} = \frac{\mathbb{E}[R_t - R_f]} {\sigma(R_t - R_f)} = \frac{\mathbb{E}[R_t]} {\sigma(R_t)} $$

A high Sharpe means the strategy produces consistent, volatility-adjusted profits.
A negative Sharpe indicates that the strategy loses money relative to its own risk.

How to interpret

  • Sharpe > 1.0 strong, risk-efficient strategy
  • 0 < Sharpe < 1 weak signal, marginally usable
  • Sharpe < 0 risk-destructive (loses money on a risk-adjusted basis)

Results in this project

All directional ML strategies yield negative Sharpe ratios.

This means:

  • The strategies are not economically viable, even if some models classify correctly more often than chance.
  • Prediction streaks cause large swings in equity curves, making the performance unstable.
  • The models fail to convert classification accuracy into a real, risk-adjusted value.
  • Volatility introduced by position switching outweighs the profits obtained.

Conclusion

Directional strategies based on these ML models do not produce positive risk-adjusted returns. Even when statistical metrics appear encouraging, the underlying predictive signal is too weak and too unstable to generate profitable trading performance once volatility is taken into account.

STEP 6 ANALYTICAL SUMMARY

6.1 Purpose and Methodological Role

This module evaluated the daily directional predictability of the IBEX 35 and S&P 500 using:

  • Technical indicators (RSI, MACD, momentum, lagged returns)
  • Conditional volatility from EGARCH
  • Machinelearning classifiers: Random Forest (RF) and XGBoost (XGB)
  • A strictly time-aligned, leakage-free modelling pipeline

This module tackles a the question: Can we predict whether tomorrows return will be positive or negative with enough statistical and economic reliability to support a trading or risk-timing decision?

With the evidence obtained, the answer is no for daily horizons, both indices, and the feature set considered.

  • In some cases (RF_IBEX), accuracy is significantly below 50 %.
  • All p-values are 0.05, confirming no statistical evidence of directional predictability.

6.2.3 Confusion Matrices and Error Structure

The confusion matrices show:

  • A nearly symmetric distribution of true/false positives and negatives
  • No dominance in correctly forecasting positive or negative days
  • No structure exploitability during trending vs. mean-reverting regimes
  • This suggests that classifiers fail to capture any consistent directional bias in market behaviour.

6.2.4 Equity Curves

  • High sensitivity to consecutive prediction errors
  • Rapid erosion of cumulative returns
  • Absence of stable compounding
  • Divergence during turbulent periods (regime sensitivity)

The absence of a positive slope reinforces the conclusion: these directional signals do not translate into implementable strategies.

6.3 Technical Diagnosis

6.3.1 Absence of Data Leakage

Once the predictive label was correctly defined as direction = sign(return). The performance collapsed to realistic values.

This confirms that the pipeline is time-aligned and leakage-free, and that poor predictability is intrinsic to the data, not due to coding flaws.

6.3.2 Features and Horizon Limitations

Price-derived features (lags, RSI, MACD, EGARCH volatility) do not capture:

  • Macro information (rates, risk premiums, macro surprises)
  • Market microstructure (order flow, liquidity imbalances)
  • Sentiment (news, NLP embeddings)

Positioning or flows (CTAs, ETFs, options hedging)

At the daily horizon, noise overwhelms the signal. Most academic work finds predictability only at lower frequencies (weekly/monthly) or with alternative information sets.

6.3.3 Validation and Robustness

The temporal split is appropriate, but:

  • Purging TimeSeriesSplit would further reduce contamination
  • Regime-dependent validation would identify periods where models systematically fail
  • Additional metrics (MCC, balanced accuracy, AUC-PR) could handle class asymmetry
  • Expected Shortfall of the strategy would quantify tail sensitivity

6.4 Practical Implications

6.4.1 For Daily Trading

  • No support for any systematic long/short strategy based on next-day prediction.
  • High turnover + noisy signals = negative risk-adjusted performance.
  • Strategies become fragile: a short error streak causes significant drawdowns.

6.4.2 For Risk Management

These results strengthen earlier conclusions:

  • Volatility forecasting (EGARCH) and tail risk (VaR/ES) are more meaningful for risk decisions.
  • Predicting magnitude and distribution of returns is more feasible than predicting sign.
  • Directional ML should be reframed as a supporting tool, not an operational signal.

6.5 Limitations

  • Daily horizon dominated by noise
  • Price-volatilityonly feature set
  • No macro/sentiment/microstructure information
  • Transaction costs not incorporated (Sharpe would worsen further)
  • Single-regime model without explicit structural break adaptation

6.7 Executive Conclusion

Central finding: Machine-learning models using price-based features cannot reliably forecast next-day market direction in IBEX or SPX.

Economic implication: All directional strategies yield negative Sharpe ratios, making them non-viable for trading or allocation.

Analytical contribution: This module provides a transparent, academically rigorous assessment of directional predictability and establishes a benchmark for future enhancements.

Future improvements: Continue developing risk-centric models (EGARCH, VaR, ES, stress testing) and explore higher-frequency information or lower-frequency horizons before revisiting directional ML.
